Project Overview

This project investigates the suitability of geothermal energy for electricity generation in the UK, specifically evaluating the geothermal reservoir in Cornwall. Three different power cycles were modelled: a single flash cycle, a binary organic Rankine cycle (ORC), and a hybrid flash-binary cycle. The models were developed in MATLAB and validated using case studies from real geothermal plants.

The goal was to determine which cycle performs best in terms of energy output and thermal efficiency, using CoolProp to source accurate thermodynamic properties of various working fluids.

Objectives

  • Model three geothermal power cycles: Flash, Binary, and Hybrid
  • Validate models using global case studies (Cerro Prieto, Miravalles)
  • Conduct a case study on the United Downs Deep Geothermal Project (UDDGP)
  • Identify the optimal working fluid and operating conditions for efficiency

Thermodynamic Background

Each cycle is a variation of the Rankine cycle, which includes processes like isentropic expansion in turbines and isobaric heat exchange. The binary cycle uses organic working fluids like n-pentane or R245fa, which allow operation at lower geothermal temperatures.

In the hybrid cycle, exhaust steam from the flash turbine is used to preheat the binary cycle fluid through a recuperator, improving overall efficiency. This method allows greater energy extraction from the geofluid before re-injection.

Case Study: Cornwall, UK

Cornwall has elevated geothermal gradients due to heat-producing granites. The UDDGP in Redruth targets reservoir temperatures of 150–200°C at depths of 5–7 km. Models were built around technical parameters of this site, including a geofluid temperature of 175°C at the production well.

The working fluid R218 was initially considered due to local plans, but n-pentane and cyclopentane outperformed it in terms of both work output and thermal efficiency.

Results and Discussion

Flash Cycle

Produced a peak of ~350 kW at a pressure drop of 32%. However, it required high geofluid temperatures and flow rates, making it unsuitable for Cornwall's relatively moderate conditions.

Binary Cycle

Reached 1.8–2 MW using R218, with the best performance at low injection well temperatures and high turbine inlet pressures. n-pentane and cyclopentane provided even better efficiency.

Hybrid Flash BinaryCycle

Combined both cycles for a peak output of 2.07 MW and thermal efficiency of ~14%. While complexity increases, it offered the most sustainable long-term performance due to enhanced heat recovery.

Key Insight:

Higher efficiency is critical for sustainability — hybrid cycles allow better long-term resource use, delaying temperature drawdown in Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS) like Cornwall's.

Tools and Technologies

  • MATLAB for model development and data visualization
  • CoolProp for accurate fluid property calculations
  • Parametric studies with contour and bar plots
  • Validation with international case studies (Mexico and Costa Rica)

Conclusion

The hybrid flash-binary cycle is the most suitable configuration for geothermal power generation in the UK based on performance, efficiency, and adaptability. While more complex, its greater energy output and efficient heat recovery make it a better long-term investment, especially in Enhanced Geothermal Systems like UDDGP.

Future improvements could include the use of optimization algorithms and development of a user interface for broader usability by engineers and researchers.
